<h2>
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor" href="#description" id="user-content-description"><svg aria-hidden="true" class="octicon octicon-link" height="16" version="1.1" viewBox="0 0 16 16" width="16"><path d="M4 9h1v1H4c-1.5 0-3-1.69-3-3.5S2.55 3 4 3h4c1.45 0 3 1.69 3 3.5 0 1.41-.91 2.72-2 3.25V8.59c.58-.45 1-1.27 1-2.09C10 5.22 8.98 4 8 4H4c-.98 0-2 1.22-2 2.5S3 9 4 9zm9-3h-1v1h1c1 0 2 1.22 2 2.5S13.98 12 13 12H9c-.98 0-2-1.22-2-2.5 0-.83.42-1.64 1-2.09V6.25c-1.09.53-2 1.84-2 3.25C6 11.31 7.55 13 9 13h4c1.45 0 3-1.69 3-3.5S14.5 6 13 6z" fill-rule="evenodd"></path></svg></a>Description</h2>
<p>This is a game inspired from the classic Tron game and Mario Kart. However, this is a twist from the aforementioned games. Each player has three lives. A player loses a life if he/she runs into the other player's trail. Note that a player is allowed to run into their own trail and that there are no “walls” in this game, i.e. if a player goes “beyond” the screen, the player will reappear on the left or the right of the screen or the top or the bottom of the screen, depending on where the player “exited” the screen from.</p>

<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor" href="#features" id="user-content-features"><svg aria-hidden="true" class="octicon octicon-link" height="16" version="1.1" viewBox="0 0 16 16" width="16"><path d="M4 9h1v1H4c-1.5 0-3-1.69-3-3.5S2.55 3 4 3h4c1.45 0 3 1.69 3 3.5 0 1.41-.91 2.72-2 3.25V8.59c.58-.45 1-1.27 1-2.09C10 5.22 8.98 4 8 4H4c-.98 0-2 1.22-2 2.5S3 9 4 9zm9-3h-1v1h1c1 0 2 1.22 2 2.5S13.98 12 13 12H9c-.98 0-2-1.22-2-2.5 0-.83.42-1.64 1-2.09V6.25c-1.09.53-2 1.84-2 3.25C6 11.31 7.55 13 9 13h4c1.45 0 3-1.69 3-3.5S14.5 6 13 6z" fill-rule="evenodd"></path></svg></a>Features</h2>
<ul>
<li>Two players can play this game</li>
<li>Each player has a total of 3 lives</li>
<li>The player can stop his/her kart</li>
<li>The player can pick up two power ups (which appear randomly on game-screen): speed-up and longer trail</li>
<li>Background game music and sound effects for player collision</li>
<li>Visual animation for player collision</li>
</ul>

<h3>
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor" href="#first-week-of-development" id="user-content-first-week-of-development"><svg aria-hidden="true" class="octicon octicon-link" height="16" version="1.1" viewBox="0 0 16 16" width="16"><path d="M4 9h1v1H4c-1.5 0-3-1.69-3-3.5S2.55 3 4 3h4c1.45 0 3 1.69 3 3.5 0 1.41-.91 2.72-2 3.25V8.59c.58-.45 1-1.27 1-2.09C10 5.22 8.98 4 8 4H4c-.98 0-2 1.22-2 2.5S3 9 4 9zm9-3h-1v1h1c1 0 2 1.22 2 2.5S13.98 12 13 12H9c-.98 0-2-1.22-2-2.5 0-.83.42-1.64 1-2.09V6.25c-1.09.53-2 1.84-2 3.25C6 11.31 7.55 13 9 13h4c1.45 0 3-1.69 3-3.5S14.5 6 13 6z" fill-rule="evenodd"></path></svg></a>First Week of Development</h3>
<p>During the first week of development, our team initially searched for prospective modules on <a href="https://www.python.org/" rel="nofollow">https://www.python.org/</a>, eventually deciding upon using the pygame module. Due to the limitation of using the pygame module on python 2.7, we looked for a way to be able to implement it with python 3.4, ultimately achieving our goal.</p>
<p>Subsequently, we followed some tutorials on youtube from an account named “sentdex”, whom you may find on the following link: <a href="https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCfzlCWGWYyIQ0aLC5w48gBQ" rel="nofollow">https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCfzlCWGWYyIQ0aLC5w48gBQ</a>.</p>
<p>Afterwards, we started developing the game’s logic, such as what determines the validity of a collision between two objects. This was achieved mainly thanks to the research of one of our team members who led the team to the answer by suggesting an analysis of the logic behind the classic Snake game.</p>
<h3>
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor" href="#second-week-of-development" id="user-content-second-week-of-development"><svg aria-hidden="true" class="octicon octicon-link" height="16" version="1.1" viewBox="0 0 16 16" width="16"><path d="M4 9h1v1H4c-1.5 0-3-1.69-3-3.5S2.55 3 4 3h4c1.45 0 3 1.69 3 3.5 0 1.41-.91 2.72-2 3.25V8.59c.58-.45 1-1.27 1-2.09C10 5.22 8.98 4 8 4H4c-.98 0-2 1.22-2 2.5S3 9 4 9zm9-3h-1v1h1c1 0 2 1.22 2 2.5S13.98 12 13 12H9c-.98 0-2-1.22-2-2.5 0-.83.42-1.64 1-2.09V6.25c-1.09.53-2 1.84-2 3.25C6 11.31 7.55 13 9 13h4c1.45 0 3-1.69 3-3.5S14.5 6 13 6z" fill-rule="evenodd"></path></svg></a>Second Week of Development</h3>
<p>In the second week of development, we started thinking about features that the game could include. We decided that the game should include the features mentioned above as well as not having an infinite trail for any player.</p>
<p>In comparison to the first week, this is where we did most of the actual coding of the game. Notably, one of our team members suggested using an object-oriented-paradigm (OOP) for our game. This led us forward in the development of the game as introducing this OOP allowed for the creation of objects to which we could add numerous features. As a result, this provided an effective way of structuring our program.</p>
<p>Furthermore, during this week, many visual and audio features were added to the game. This included the addition of icons for the power-ups (designed by our team), a background for the game-screen, background music, game-event sounds, visual effects and notably, the implementation of the trail of each player’s “kart” and the players' “kart” itself.</p>
